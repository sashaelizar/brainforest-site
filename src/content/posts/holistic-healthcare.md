id="main-problems-with-modern-healthcare-dnvj0"></span><span><span>Main problems with modern healthcare </span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-2qckt"><span><span>The current Western medical paradigm has been heavily shaped by industrialization. Some major problems with healthcare include:</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-19s9s"><span id="1-reactive-and-episodic-19s9s"></span><span><span>1. Reactive and episodic</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-fj8fl"><span><span>Health is not a one-off deal but a continual pursuit. Patients typically present to the doctor with a bothersome symptom such as pain, and they are promptly prescribed medication without much thought. Instead, healthcare should be proactive, preventive, and emphasize lifestyle interventions.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-87l1q"><span id="2-over-prescription-87l1q"></span><span><span>2. Over-prescription</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-80oqv"><span><span>The opioid epidemic, antibiotic overuse, and other medical practices have contributed to chronic disease. 17% of Americans take 3 or more prescriptions. These are often last-generation, expensive, quick fixes that manage symptoms, have unpleasant side effects, and don't functionally target the disease drivers or health promoters. Functional, personalized, precision molecular medicines are needed.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-fpgea"><div><figure><a href="https://www.cdc.gov/antibiotic-use/community/programs-measurement/measuring-antibiotic-prescribing.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><div><div id="fpgea"><wow-image id="fa29ad_43fad073f7f54ef89d2555ac0df81251~mv2_d_3300_2550_s_4_2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/02_fa29ad-43fad073f7f54ef89d2555ac0df81251-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></a><div id="viewer-fpgea-caption"><div><figcaption><span>Source: CDC</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-f0o2t"><span id="3-medical-error-f0o2t"></span><span><span>3. Medical error</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-3p9k7"><span><span>Doctors and other HCPs often work over 8-hour shifts, with 12- or even 16-hour shifts being common practice among </span><a href="https://www.americannursetoday.com/blog/are-12-hour-shifts-safe/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>nurses</span></u></span></a><span>. Fatigue and stress increase the likelihood of mistakes and decrease patient satisfaction (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3608421/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Stimpfel, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>). Working shifts longer than 13 hours can triple the chance of medical error (</span><a href="https://www.jointcommission.org/assets/1/18/S2-JQPS-11-07S-lockley.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Lockley, 2007</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-739vo"><span><span>According to some sources, medical error is the third leading cause of death in the U.S. (</span><a href="https://www.bmj.com/content/353/bmj.i2139.full" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Makary, 2016</span></u></span></a><span>). One study estimated that in the U.S., 210,000-440,000 deaths per year are due to medical error (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23860193" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>James, 2013</span></u></span></a><span>). Medical error can include errors in diagnosis, treatment strategy, or prevention. To have any hope of treating and preventing illnesses, we need to advance our causal models of the human body and uphold ethical occupational health standards for the healthcare workforce. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-dr8pt"><div><figure><div><div id="dr8pt"><wow-image id="fa29ad_77962d5b8377483da80ff0d501ae24f5~mv2.png"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/04_fa29ad-77962d5b8377483da80ff0d501ae24f5-.png"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-6fsc"><span id="4-impersonal-6fsc"></span><span><span>4. Impersonal</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-81nbl"><span><span>Doctors often have busy schedules, leaving little time for patient interactions. This can cause them to not listen as attentively, ask the right questions, or take their patients' concerns seriously. This is problematic because the patient is more familiar with their body and has more information and investment in their health than a third-party observer. The reality is, when interaction is minimized to a mere 5-10 minutes, the relationship doesn't have much room to grow. This rift can increase patient non-compliance or cause patients to switch healthcare providers.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-1nff7"><span id="5-inefficient-centralized-data-sharing-1nff7"></span><span><span>5. Inefficient centralized data sharing </span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-ejvb8"><span><span>Although there is a massive amount of data, we still lack implementation of reliable, user-friendly data centralization platforms. Electronic medical records (EMRs) often don't have enough of the "right kind" of data that matters to the patient. Doctors don't have access to lifestyle data, and patients sometimes have poor access to their own medical records. When lifestyle data from health trackers like FitBit is sent to medical professionals, that data becomes </span><a href="https://healthitsecurity.com/news/how-does-hipaa-apply-to-wearable-health-technology" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>HIPAA-protected</span></u></span></a><span>, creating a gray area with regards to data integration and privacy.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-dm806"><span><span>In addition, even though you're entitled to access to your own medical records, some patients are </span><a href="https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/insights/daniel-p-kessler-hidden-cost-changing-doctors" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>charged</span></u></span></a><span> for </span><a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/nextavenue/2017/02/09/are-you-paying-too-much-for-your-medical-records/#dcb698f6a2be" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>access to</span></u></span></a><span> or copies of their own medical records. In New Jersey, for example, it is legal for health care providers to charge up to </span><a href="https://www.lamblawoffice.com/new-jersey-medical-records-copying-charges-law-statute/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>$200</span></u></span></a><span> for hard copies of one's medical record, which can make it more difficult to switch doctors. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-a57ob"><span><span>Now that we have identified some current limitations of the medical industry, let's talk about opportunities for growth in healthcare.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-bp9gr"><div><figure><div><div id="bp9gr"><wow-image id="fa29ad_df992bcfb2d04134949b8f427085489c~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/10_fa29ad-df992bcfb2d04134949b8f427085489c-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-b37td"><span id="future-directions-of-functional-medicine-b37td"></span><span><span>Future Directions of Functional Medicine</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-q84k"><span><span>Some promising avenues of medicine include:</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-2vtsu"><span id="1-ai-and-machine-learning-in-diagnostics-2vtsu"></span><span><span>1. AI and machine learning in diagnostics</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-5am1g"><span><span>Artificial intelligence (AI) could manage mountains of data, even unstructured ones like brain scans and voice recordings. If you can teach a computer pairings between charts and disease states, you can quickly pin down possible diagnoses based on imaging such as MRI, PET, CT, angiography, x-rays, next-gen dental digital imaging, or DEXA bone density scans, bloodwork, and other biometric data. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-7pl0n"><div><figure><div><div id="7pl0n"><wow-image id="fa29ad_2ad3e67dbab14fa486c2f0483048d23b~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/01_fa29ad-2ad3e67dbab14fa486c2f0483048d23b-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-fn7mo"><span id="2-multi-omic-sequencing-for-personalized-disease-prevention-and-disease-modifying-lead-generation-fn7mo"></span><span><span>2. Multi-omic sequencing for personalized disease prevention and disease-modifying lead generation</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-f1m48"><span><span>Information on mutations that affect absorption (e.g. in certain intestinal cell transporters) could allow doctors to advise patients on dietary or nutraceutical interventions. Sequencing could also identify genetic risk factors and help personalize lifestyle interventions. Genome, proteome, metabolome, and microbiome sequencing methods like RNA-seq and GeoMX digital spatial profiling can reveal the role of environmental factors in molecular and cellular function. Sequencing methods are becoming increasingly cheaper and more efficient. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-68jm1"><div><figure><div><div id="68jm1"><wow-image id="fa29ad_771f9983a1b34c66ad735e98786dcba3~mv2.png"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/03_fa29ad-771f9983a1b34c66ad735e98786dcba3-.png"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-6muum"><span id="3-biologics-and-nutraceuticals-6muum"></span><span><span>3. Biologics and nutraceuticals</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-6jt1g"><span><span>Plants, fungi, prebiotics, probiotics, postbiotics, and synbiotics will be key xenobiotic components of nutraceuticals. These therapeutics will be enhanced with R&amp;D in science and biotechnology. Biotechnology solutions require skill in manufacturing, packaging, processing, and delivery. Training in biology, biomedical engineering, physics, and chemistry are in great demand. Areas of growing interest include biomaterials, 3D printing, formulations, and synthesis of biodegradable polymers, micelles, and nanoparticles to enhance absorption and therapeutic value.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-bhd2r"><div><figure><div><div id="bhd2r"><wow-image id="fa29ad_b57be560da744b459dee7a005db35015~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/07_fa29ad-b57be560da744b459dee7a005db35015-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-1u7ip"><span id="4-minimally-invasive-surgery-and-advanced-medical-devices-1u7ip"></span><span><span>4. Minimally-invasive surgery and advanced medical devices </span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-f0uq5"><span><span>Surgical techniques are improving, and as a result, surgical scars are shrinking. Nanotechnology allows the ingestion of tiny monitoring devices that can assist in diagnostics and surgical interventions. Medical innovations and scientific findings are cutting down on unnecessary procedures, and the risks and benefits are being more thoroughly analyzed and conveyed. AI is also being used in </span><a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/bernardmarr/2018/07/27/how-is-ai-used-in-healthcare-5-powerful-real-world-examples-that-show-the-latest-advances/#5a57a72c5dfb" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>robotic surgery</span></u></span></a><span> to minimize potential complications and postoperative pain. This shift towards decreased invasiveness will continue as surgical techniques advance.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-b4jis"><span><span>On a related note, new technologies like </span><a href="http://dia-vit.com/#np-119" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Dia-Vit</span></u></span></a><span>, </span><a href="http://www.gluco-wise.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>GlucoWise</span></u></span></a><span>, and </span><a href="https://nemauramedical.com/sugarbeat/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>sugarBEAT</span></u></span></a><span> allow patients with diabetes to accurately track their blood glucose level without the painful finger prick. Products are undergoing testing, and the ones out on the market may still be expensive.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-f9kbr"><span id="5-expanded-healthcare-ecosystem-to-include-functional-medicine-practitioners-board-certified-in-nutrition-f9kbr"></span><span><span>5. Expanded healthcare ecosystem to include functional medicine practitioners board-certified in nutrition</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-64tle"><span><span>Allied health professions include medical doctors (MD), osteopathic doctors (DO), dentists (DDS), chiropractors (DC), naturopathic doctors (ND), physician assistants (PA), nurses (RN or NP), and physical therapists (PT). Informative, user-friendly digital interface</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-dehl6"><span><span>Healthcare providers, researchers, and patients need to articulate their needs and identify what's missing from current technology. IT experts need to understand the needs of users and come up with and implement creative solutions.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-43kbs"><div><figure><div><div id="43kbs"><wow-image id="fa29ad_a84bfa7cbd754806a8ace8943aa29352~mv2_d_1785_1379_s_2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/holistic-healthcare/06_fa29ad-a84bfa7cbd754806a8ace8943aa29352-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-2gcb4"><span><span>Patients want something consistent that will allow them to quickly and conveniently log lifestyle factors such as diet, exercise, sleep, water intake, health conditions, and menstrual cycles. Wearable health devices, such as FitBit or Apple Watch, already offer this technology. The next step is more advanced monitoring, such as skin cortisol levels, and widespread integration. If wearable health devices are synced with electronic medical records, the data could be assembled into a more comprehensive picture of patient health. Data could also be used, with patient consent, to make new findings that could directly benefit the patient and other people.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-2d3ar"><span><span>Health insurance companies could also incentivize preventive lifestyle behaviors through fitness trackers, offering lower rates for individuals as their biometric data indicates more exercise, better sleep, or otherwise increasing health. Vibrational technologies employing electromagnetic (light) and sound frequencies are being used as innovative cancer treatments, with lower side effects and toxicity to the patient than chemotherapy and radiation. Surgery is becoming less invasive and less necessary.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-2rb7q"><span><span>Healthcare practice is expanding as an ecosystem of professionals all with investment in patient and consumer health. Overall, all these changes are expected to advance public health for the better.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div></div><div type="last"></div></div></div></div></div></div></section>
