id="meet-your-second-brain-and-endocrine-organ-4g99o"></span><span><span>Meet your second brain and endocrine organ </span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-cvadq"><span><span>The enteric nervous system, the network of neurons innervating the GI tract, has been called the second brain because it's the largest component of the autonomic nervous system, numbering around 100 million neurons. The vagus nerve runs from the brain stem to the heart, lungs, gut, and other organs. This is one key way the microbiome communicates with the brain, apart from releasing metabolites into systemic circulation.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-66nac"><span><span>The gut microbiota has been proposed by scientists to be designated as an endocrine organ because of its widespread interactions with host physiology. Gut microbes can produce neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine.[4] They can also neutralize carcinogens and control oxidative stress and inflammatory responses.[5]</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-7r9a6"><div><figure><div><div id="7r9a6"><wow-image id="fa29ad_772ed3444e564c24909ffae44e22f54b~mv2.png"><img alt="vagus nerve diagram of connections to organs" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/07_fa29ad-772ed3444e564c24909ffae44e22f54b-.png"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-5fb7i"><span id="6-benefits-of-exercise-on-gut-brain-health-5fb7i"></span><span><span>6 Benefits of Exercise on Gut-Brain Health</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-4s445"><span id="1-exercise-increases-serotonin-and-dopamine-improving-mood-4s445"></span><span><span>1. Exercise increases serotonin and dopamine, improving mood</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-atmrb"><span><span>You may notice increased energy and mental clarity after a great workout. This is no coincidence: exercise increases serotonin and dopamine levels in the brain.[5] Rats that swam briefly and regularly had increased levels of serotonin in the brainstem and hypothalamus.[6] Running at low speed also increased brain serotonin and decreased depressive and anxious behavior in rats.[7] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-f6ros"><span id="2-exercise-increases-satiety-and-decreases-hunger-f6ros"></span><span><span>2. Exercise increases satiety and decreases hunger</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-eh4li"><span><span>Light to moderate exercise can make you feel less hungry, thanks to the interplay between gut microbes and hormones. Exercise increases the abundance of two mutualistic bacterial genera, </span><em><span>Bifidobacterium</span></em><span> and </span><em><span>Lactobacillus, </span></em><span>which are both associated with increased level of leptin, a satiety hormone. Meanwhile, exercise decreases secretion of ghrelin, a hunger hormone associated with increased </span><em><span>Bacteroides </span></em><span>and </span><em><span>Prevotella.</span></em><span>[8, 9] Exercise was found to decrease insulin, IL-6, and VLDL secretion.[9]</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-7mue4"><div><figure><div><div id="7mue4"><wow-image id="fa29ad_8fb204d9deac4127a16f0809a790a14b~mv2.png"><img alt="balance of leptin and ghrelin before and after meals" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/08_fa29ad-8fb204d9deac4127a16f0809a790a14b-.png"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-495bb"><span id="3-exercise-stimulates-gi-motility-which-improves-intestinal-integrity-495bb"></span><span><span>3. Exercise stimulates GI motility, which improves intestinal integrity</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-fh8gd"><span><span>Exercise also indirectly preserves the gut barrier by stimulating GI motility. By reducing transient stool time, exercise minimizes contact between potential pathogens and the GI mucosa. This partly explains the decreased risk of colon cancer, diverticulosis, IBD, and hemorrhage in individuals who exercise. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-35hgh"><span id="4-exercise-kept-villi-thinner-a-sign-of-lower-gut-inflammation-35hgh"></span><span><span>4. Exercise kept villi thinner, a sign of lower gut inflammation</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-bo7kg"><span><span>A team at Rutgers University investigated the effect of diet (normal vs. high-fat diet (HFD)) and exercise (active vs. sedentary) on inflammation, intestinal permeability, and microbiota. They found obese (HFD) sedentary mice had twice the gut villi width of lean sedentary animals. A major cause of villi widening was an increase in inflammatory cells in lymph and plasma cells and fat cells. The HFD exercised animals had normal villi.[2] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-a6s3o"><span id="5-exercise-reduced-cox2-expression-in-the-gut-a6s3o"></span><span><span>5. Exercise reduced Cox2 expression in the gut</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-c60dl"><span><span>The same Rutgers study looked at the expression of an enzyme called Cox2 that produces prostaglandins, which promotes inflammation, pain, and fever. Obese sedentary mice had the highest Cox2 expression, followed by lean sedentary and lean exercised mice. Surprisingly, obese exercised mice had the lowest Cox2 expression.[2] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-89tqt"><span><span>This suggested that not only is a sedentary lifestyle a risk factor for inflammation even in lean individuals, but also that exercise can make a huge difference for obese individuals eating a high-fat diet.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-81d00"><span id="6-exercise-upregulates-antioxidant-enzymes-and-has-anti-inflammatory-pro-survival-effect-in-intestinal-lymphocytes-81d00"></span><span><span>6. Exercise upregulates antioxidant enzymes and has anti-inflammatory, pro-survival effect in intestinal lymphocytes</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-99bl1"><span><span>Exercise increased expression of glutathione peroxidase and catalase, anti-inflammatory cytokine IL-10, and pro-survival protein Bcl-2 in intestinal lymphocytes. This promoted the survival of intestinal lymphocytes and increased their numbers. By the same token, exercise decreased expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines TNF-alpha and IL-17, apoptotic proteins caspases 3 and 7, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation in intestinal lymphocytes.[10]</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-ffhqb"><div><figure><div><div id="ffhqb"><wow-image id="fa29ad_93a49c6a30ba4a1da326bae35a6f103b~mv2.jpg"><img alt="girl doing planking exercise" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/09_fa29ad-93a49c6a30ba4a1da326bae35a6f103b-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-67f0u"><span id="how-do-gut-microbiota-differ-in-athletes-and-sedentary-individuals-67f0u"></span><span><span>How do gut microbiota differ in athletes and sedentary individuals?</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-a46j6"><span><span>Exercise changes the diversity and composition of gut microbiota in specific ways. For example, athletes have higher levels of butyrate and increased </span><em><span>Faecalibacterium prausnitzii </span></em><span>and </span><em><span>Akkermansia muciniphila</span></em><span>, while sedentary individuals have higher </span><em><span>Proteobacteria </span></em><span>and </span><em><span>Acidobacteria.</span></em></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-5mnd5"><span id="1-exercise-enriches-biodiversity-5mnd5"></span><span><span>1. Exercise enriches biodiversity</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-du6su"><span><span>Low microbial diversity is associated with more adiposity, insulin resistance, and inflammation; it's also a risk factor for weight gain. Exercise such as running can enhance the number of beneficial microbial species and improve host health.[9, 11] HIIT was also found to increase alpha diversity in mice; this effect was more pronounced in the distal gut, where it makes a difference in colon health.[12] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-25obj"><div><figure><div><div id="25obj"><wow-image id="fa29ad_3ae857071dbe49fb8e78218671d53e16~mv2.jpg"><img alt="colorized electron microscopy of gut microbiota" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/05_fa29ad-3ae857071dbe49fb8e78218671d53e16-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-cfipu"><span id="2-exercise-expands-butyrate-producing-bacteria-cfipu"></span><span><span>2. Exercise expands butyrate-producing bacteria</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-8sfg9"><span><span>Certain bacteria convert plant-based carbohydrates (fiber) into short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), an indicator of gut health. SCFAs like acetate, propionate, butyrate enhance barrier function, reduce inflammation, provide energy for colonocytes, prevent mucosal degradation, and overall protect the GI tract.[9] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-2p3c"><span><span>SCFA-producing bacteria include </span><em><span>Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Clostridium, Bacteroides, Roseburia, </span></em><span>and</span><em><span> Lachnospiraceae</span></em><span>.[9] In particular, </span><em><span>Lactobacillus</span></em><span> and </span><em><span>Bifidobacterium</span></em><span> enhance absorption of vitamins and minerals.[13] Exercise, including cardio, has been found to increase numbers and diversity of these butyrate producers.[9, 14]</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5duph"><span><span>Higher </span><em><span>Faecalibacterium prausnitzii</span></em><span> was found in elite rugby players as well as in female athletes.[11] Additionally, female athletes had more </span><em><span>Roseburia hominis</span></em><span> and</span><em><span> Prevotella</span></em><span>.[15] When levels of these bacteria increase, so do levels of SCFAs and their associated benefits.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-16se6"><div><figure><div><div id="16se6"><wow-image id="fa29ad_0062ab9c44ab4e6ab9fec7ed7696cc6e~mv2.jpg"><img alt="good and bad bacterial flora" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/01_fa29ad-0062ab9c44ab4e6ab9fec7ed7696cc6e-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-8q6ih"><span id="3-exercise-expands-mucus-degrading-akkermansia-muciniphila-8q6ih"></span><span><span>3. Exercise expands mucus-degrading </span><em><span>Akkermansia muciniphila</span></em></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-a6rg8"><span><span>Athletes (both men and women) have higher mucus-degrading </span><em><span>Akkermansia muciniphila,</span></em><span>[16] which improves mucus turnover and helps to maintain the gut barrier.[15] A substantial body of literature suggests </span><em><span>A. muciniphila</span></em><span> plays a role in mucosal homeostasis. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-8j191"><span><span>On the flipside,</span><em><span> A. muciniphila</span></em><span> is also elevated in patients with multiple sclerosis that present with leaky gut. One possible reason is excessively thin mucus and insufficient turnover. Conversely, thick mucus (caused by inflammatory diet or infection) can also increase levels of pathogenic mucus degraders. Overall, when it comes to gut mucosa, it’s all about balance and homeostatic turnover.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-28rcv"><div><figure><div><div id="28rcv"><wow-image id="fa29ad_209cacee0eea479f89af67942d0edd92~mv2.jpg"><img alt="colorized electron microscopy of bacteria" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/04_fa29ad-209cacee0eea479f89af67942d0edd92-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-flo03"><span id="4-sedentary-lifestyle-increases-inflammatory-proteobacteria-and-acidobacteria-flo03"></span><span><span>4. Sedentary lifestyle increases inflammatory </span><em><span>Proteobacteria</span></em><span> and </span><em><span>Acidobacteria</span></em></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-f2ajf"><span><span>Sedentary individuals may have higher levels of pathogenic, inflammatory </span><em><span>Proteobacteria </span></em><span>compared to active individuals.[17]</span><em><span> Acidobacteria </span></em><span>was also found to be higher in sedentary women.[15] Additionally, the beneficial butyrate-producing bacteria, </span><em><span>Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, </span></em><span>was high in active mice but not found in sedentary mice.[2] Overall, poor diet, sedentary lifestyle, antibiotics, and other factors can lower biodiversity, which increases risk of pathogenic bacteria dominating the gut.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-cr2f1"><span id="when-intense-exercise-becomes-gut-stress-when-to-not-exercise-cr2f1"></span><span><span>When intense exercise becomes gut stress &amp; when to not exercise</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-ecvk3"><span><span>Running is known for its adrenaline rush. But prolonged stressful, fight-or-flight situations release norepinephrine and cortisol, which have inhibitory effects on the gut. Endurance cardio increases blood flow to the muscles, heart, and lungs and decreases blood flow to the small and large intestines. This decreased blood flow to the gut can increase tight junction opening, resulting in leaky gut. Leaky gut, potentially caused by dehydration, allows bacterial LPS translocation, which triggers the host's immune system to release inflammatory molecules like TNF-a, IFN-gamma, IL-1B, histamine, and proteases.[18, 19] The process can continue and lead to endotoxemia. In fact, marathon runners and triathletes frequently report symptoms like nausea, cramping, vomiting, or diarrhea.[5] However, it is possible that this effect could be mitigated with adequate training. Decreased blood flow to the gut was shown in foxhounds to wear off after 8-12 weeks of training.[20] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5rb74"><span><span>Exercising to exhaustion is not recommended because it temporarily weakens the immune system and increases inflammation.[5] This is why individuals fighting a cold are advised to wait about 48 hours for recovery before returning to the gym. Patients with chronic fatigue syndrome show worsening gut dysbiosis, inflammation, pain, and mood following exercise.[21] Overall, the more strenuous the exercise and the more depleted the energy reserves, the greater the barrier disruption, due to changes in blood flow and insufficient removal of metabolites and delivery of nutrients.[2] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-djvh1"><div><figure><div><div id="djvh1"><wow-image id="fa29ad_0de2ffd1199f402b952c47cd9b842312~mv2.jpg"><img alt="buff woman lifting weights" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/03_fa29ad-0de2ffd1199f402b952c47cd9b842312-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-3lvm0"><span id="how-do-resistance-training-calisthenics-stretching-and-yoga-affect-gut-microbiota-3lvm0"></span><span><span>How do resistance training, calisthenics, stretching, and yoga affect gut microbiota? </span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-1g3fc"><span><span>Type and intensity of exercise clearly matters, but to my knowledge, there are no published peer-reviewed studies on the effects of strength training on gut microbiota, indicating an open area for research. Muscle mass has previously correlated to several bacterial populations, so it's likely there's an effect. More clinical studies are needed to establish data relevant to humans and their diverse activities.[15] </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-95fjd"><div><figure><div><div id="95fjd"><wow-image id="fa29ad_01a91b9fffa84e6994b72f04a1306b78~mv2_d_2441_2746_s_4_2.jpg"><img alt="known causes of leaky gut" loading="lazy" src="/images/exercise-gut-health/02_fa29ad-01a91b9fffa84e6994b72f04a1306b78-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div><div id="viewer-95fjd-caption"><div><figcaption><span>Source: Dr. Peter Osborne, Gluten Free Society</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-f1pq4"><span id="key-takeaways-f1pq4"></span><span><span>Key takeaways</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-9hd0g"><span><strong><span>Gut microbiota are affected by</span></strong><span> infection, disease, diet, antibiotics, exercise, which can in turn modulate disease.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5palf"><span><strong><span>Early-life interventions have a profound effect. </span></strong><span>Beneficial effects of exercise on the gut and brain were more profound when started earlier in life.[22]</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5vgh2"><span><strong><span>Recovery is necessary. </span></strong><span>It’s important to distinguish between physical and psychological stress in exercise, especially in endurance sports.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-ej2o7"><span><strong><span>There is no one optimal diet</span></strong><span> because of high interindividual variability in gut microbiota, genetics, exposure, and lifestyle. Despite this, functions carried out by these microbial species appear to be the same in everyone's GI tracts.[23] Therefore, it is possible to personalize a diet that is optimal for an individual at a given time.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5epd1"><span><strong><span>Gut microbiota research is still young. </span></strong><span>There are thousands of species. We're still discovering new gut bacterial species, and certain species can be beneficial in one context and opportunistic pathogens in another. Thanks to horizontal gene transfer, they can also evolve rapidly, giving rise to new strains.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-fqv04"><span><strong><span>We need diversity. </span></strong><span>Having a more diverse gut microbiota decreases the likelihood of illness, as they keep each other in check.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-2qfmf"><span><strong><span>Diet</span></strong><span> still impacts gut microbiota more than exercise, but exercise can have profound effects on gut health and gut microbial composition as well.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-3g2b6"><div><figure><div><div id="3g2b6"><wow-image id="fa29ad_6b8af9d7eb804ed2bcd65b129a51d34d~mv2.jpg"><img
