Buy organic to avoid many of the contaminant exposures.</span></figcaption></div></div><div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-te18x1680922"><span><span>It’s worth noting some foods naturally contain high levels of toxic chemicals, for example, tuna and rice (heavy metals), farmed salmon (plasticizers), cassava (cyanide-releasing compounds, if not properly prepared), processed meats (nitrates), and fried foods (e.g. potato chips and fries – carcinogenic acrylamides).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-4ucoc1673926"><span><span><span>For a more detailed discussion on the role of pesticides (e.g. atrazine, organophosphorus, and chlorpyrifos), plastics (including BPA, styrofoam, bioplastics), rinse aids, and PoPs in gut health, see</span></span> <a href="/ebook/leaky-gut-causes"><u><span>the full-length guide.</span></u></a></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><ol start="7"><li><h2 id="viewer-3t7a0146504"><span id="alcohol-cigarettes-opiates-and-other-psychoactive-substances-3t7a0146"></span><span><span><span>Alcohol, Cigarettes, Opiates, and Other Psychoactive Substances</span></span></span></h2></li></ol></div><div type="ordered_list"></div><div><p id="viewer-oefzx146507"><span><span><span>Alcohol is known to cause intestinal permeability and can also cause hormone and neurotransmitter imbalance and contribute to the risk of breast cancer </span></span><span>[8]</span><span><span>. </span></span><span><span>The ethanol metabolite acetaldehyde also contributes to leaky gut [46].</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-3bzlq1691231"><span><span><span>Chronic alcohol use disorder has been shown to lead to intestinal permeability via microbiota dysbiosis. Not all chronic alcohol misuse leads to leaky gut; it appears that both microbiota dysbiosis and alcohol misuse are necessary to induce intestinal permeability. Within two weeks of abstaining from alcohol, intestinal permeability is restored, but microbiota dysbiosis may persist. This also shows that microbiota dysbiosis can exist without leaky gut [47].</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-fs5wr1691235"><span><span><span>Morphine and fentanyl have also been shown to impair intestinal barrier function [48].</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-s9isx1691239"><span><span><span>Cigarette smoke and e-cigarettes have also been shown to compromise the intestinal barrier [49, 50]. </span></span><span><span>Smoking also causes gut dysbiosis.</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-7ce4s1691244"><span><span><span>According to current literature, cannabis does not impair intestinal permeability; however, more research is needed on effects of cannabis smoking on the epithelial barrier. Additionally, there is no peer-reviewed evidence that psilocybin impairs intestinal permeability. There is also not enough evidence to conclude whether stimulants such as caffeine, cocaine, or amphetamines cause leaky gut [51].</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><ol start="8"><li><h2 id="viewer-e8cs4146617"><span id="infection-toxins-in-food-and-microbial-endotoxins-e8cs4146"></span><span><span>Infection, toxins in food, and microbial endotoxins</span></span></h2></li></ol></div><div type="ordered_list"></div><div><p id="viewer-x7bae146619"><span><strong><span>Toxins in food and water can contribute to allergy development. </span></strong><span>A 2022</span><span> paper published in </span><em><span>Frontiers in Allergy</span></em><span> showed that co-exposure to toxins from microbes and food allergens sensitizes the immune system to developing food allergies by damaging the epithelial barrier. That is, mycotoxin and cholera toxin had an adjuvant effect in the development of peanut allergies [52]. Aflatoxin, a common food toxin, is found in moldy grains and nuts (peanuts, corn, and wheat).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-c6p9a1694721"><span><span><span>Specific enteropathogens can harm gut health, including bacteria and eukaryotes (fungi, protozoa, and parasites). </span></span><em><span>Bacteroides fragilis</span></em><span><span>[46, 53, 54], </span></span><em><span><span>Clostridium difficile </span></span></em><span><span>[55], </span></span><em><span><span>Staphylococcus aureus, </span></span></em><span><span>certain </span></span><em><span><span>E. coli</span></span></em><span><span> strains [46, 56, 57], certain bacteria involved in food poisoning (</span></span><em><span><span>Salmonella </span></span></em><span><span>and </span></span><em><span><span>Listeria</span></span></em><span><span>) and others have been linked to leaky gut. Various fungi (particularly </span></span><strong><em><span><span>Candida</span></span></em></strong><span><span>) </span></span><span>and parasites like </span><strong><em><span>Toxoplasma gondii</span></em></strong><span> [54] can wear down the gut lining and open tight junctions. More recent investigations are also revealing a role for </span><strong><span>bacteriophages</span></strong><span>, the viruses that infect bacteria, in intestinal permeability. </span><span><span>(See our </span></span><a href="/ebook/leaky-gut-causes"><u><span>full report</span></u></a><span><span> for a comprehensive guide and more species- and strain-specific information).</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><ol start="9"><li><h2 id="viewer-zbob8146652"><span id="medications-zbob8146"></span><span><span>Medications</span></span></h2></li></ol></div><div type="ordered_list"></div><div><p id="viewer-68n24146654"><span><span><span>Certain medications and over-the-counter drugs carry risk of GI-related side effects.</span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-gbhw01703360"><span><span><span>Medications, including</span></span><strong><span><span> antibiotics</span></span></strong><span><span>, the hormonal</span></span><strong><span><span> contraceptive</span></span></strong><span><span> pill [58] as well as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are known to increase the risk of IBD [59-61]. </span></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-1aiqy1711087"><span id="contraceptives-1aiqy171"></span><span><span>Contraceptives</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-i4tlf1711944"><span><span><span>A meta-analysis of case-controlled and cohort studies revealed that individuals exposed to oral contraceptives had a 24% and 30% increased risk for developing Crohn’s disease and ulcerative colitis, respectively, compared with those not exposed to the medication [62]. Meanwhile, normally menstruating women did not have </span></span><span>different intestinal permeability than men [63].</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-ezl6r1703381"><span id="antibiotics-ezl6r170"></span><span><span>Antibiotics</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-8u44j1747995"><span><span>Certain antibiotics, such as neomycin and bacitracin [64] may improve intestinal integrity, while others such as metronidazole [65] may weaken the mucosal barrier. Thus, the effect of the antibiotic on the gut depends on identity, dosage, and how it’s combined. Further distinctions about the effect of different types of antibiotics are available in the free </span><a href="/ebook/leaky-gut-causes"><u><span>full report</span></u></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-0ddft1747999"><span id="nsaids-0ddft174"></span><span><span>NSAIDs</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-12nlc1748001"><span><span>In 2001, GlaxoSmithKline demonstrated that indomethacin, flurbiprofen, or diclofenac increased the vascular leakage of radiolabeled albumin in the jejenum, determined 24 hours later [66]. A meta-analysis published in the </span><em><span>Journal of Gastroenterology</span></em><span> in 2009 showed that NSAIDs increased intestinal permeability [67]. Chronic use is likely more harmful than sporadic, occasional use when medically needed.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-8tqy91748005"><span id="other-drugs-8tqy9174"></span><span><span>Other Drugs</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-98e2q1748007"><span><strong><span><span>S</span></span></strong><strong><span>teroids, acid blockers,</span></strong><strong><span>hormonal replacement</span></strong><span>, </span><strong><span>antipsychotic medications,</span></strong><span> and </span><strong><span>opioids </span></strong><span>all damage the gut and cause microbiome problems and are detrimental to gut barrier function [68]. Always prioritize dietary modifications as a health buffer, but take medications when they are medically necessary.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-1vkt21748019"><span><span><span>Certain </span></span><strong><span><span>chemotherapy</span></span></strong><span><span> drugs may cause intestinal barrier injury [6, 69]</span></span><span>. Preclinical data also suggests that </span><strong><span>proton pump </span></strong><span>inhibitors increase tight junction barrier permeability [70] and damage mitochondria. Alcohol and </span><strong><span>corticosteroid</span></strong><span> (e.g. hydrocortisone) exposure may act through glucocorticoid receptors in the intestinal epithelial cells, and they have been shown to increase gut permeability [71].</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-98nhi1748030"><span><span>Recognizing certain lifesaving medications are medically necessary, an appropriate action is to mitigate the damage through epithelial-restoring foods. However, it is recommended to work with a specialized nutritionist qualified to provide recommendations in the context of certain disease diagnoses, such as cancer.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><ol start="14"><li><h2 id="viewer-jsogt798734"><span id="ionizing-radiation-jsogt798"></span><span><span>Ionizing Radiation</span></span></h2></li></ol></div><div type="ordered_list"></div><div><p id="viewer-wvmue146710"><span><span>Ionizing radiation causes intestinal injury and microbial translocation [72-78], but certain nutraceuticals demonstrate radioprotective properties, mitigating damage [79-89].
