id="oxidative-stress-a-major-driver-of-aging-related-disease-7ptlv"></span><span><span>Oxidative stress: a major driver of aging-related disease</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7qbsf"><span><span>During the conversion of food to energy to fuel cellular processes, our mitochondria naturally produce </span><strong><span>free radicals</span></strong><span> and </span><strong><span>reactive oxygen species</span></strong><span> (ROS), unstable molecules that contain unpaired electrons. ROS is a normal part of redox signaling, which gets balanced out in states of health. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-a327t"><span><span>Our bodies have their own systems for neutralizing ROS—the endogenous antioxidants, molecules that fight the formation and propagation of free radicals. These include glutathione (GSH), metal-binding proteins, and enzymes like superoxide dismutase (SOD) and glutathione peroxidase.<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup> In particular, glutathione is the predominant mammalian liver antioxidant. It detoxifies a wide range of toxic compounds, including carcinogens. Glutathione depletion can impair immune function and is associated with increased risk for cancer, cardiovascular diseases, arthritis, and diabetes.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-6r2l7"><div><figure><div><div id="6r2l7"><wow-image id="fa29ad_d058b4037a2642d0948437447f3db02a~mv2.jpg"><img alt="chart of free radicals or reactive oxygen species examples that cause oxidative stress and drive aging" loading="lazy" src="/images/ergothioneine/08_fa29ad-d058b4037a2642d0948437447f3db02a-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div><div id="viewer-6r2l7-caption"><div><figcaption><span> Examples of reactive oxygen species, some of the molecules behind aging. Others (not shown) include hypochlorous acid (HOCl), peroxynitrite (ONOO−), and nitric oxide (NO). ONOO- can cause lipid peroxidation, damage protein and DNA and deplete intracellular antioxidants; it is extensively implicated in many disorders.<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup> </span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-f943l"><span><span>ROS production can supersede the body's defense systems. These excess ROS zoom around the body looking for bonds to stabilize themselves, damaging cells, proteins, lipids, and DNA. They can disrupt enzymes and cell walls and can eventually kill cells. This process is called </span><strong><span>oxidative stress. </span></strong></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-bdpbh"><span id="causes-of-oxidative-stress-bdpbh"></span><span><span>Causes of oxidative stress</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-bh2nu"><span><span>Oxidative stress is, in part, endogenous. Free radicals form in response to normal mitochondrial metabolism. Mitochondria generate superoxide anion (O2-) and hydroxyl radicals (.OH) during respiration.<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup> Free radicals can also be generated as part of the immune response, during white blood cell-mediated inflammation. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-a9vej"><span><span>Environmental factors can also exacerbate oxidative stress. These disease drivers include poor nutrition, fried food, alcohol, smoke and smoking (regardless of what is smoked), ionizing and non-ionizing radiation, UV light, certain drugs (e.g. cisplatin), hyperoxia (too much oxygen), pesticides, and atmospheric pollution.<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-dhsjj"><span id="biomarkers-of-oxidative-damage-dhsjj"></span><span><span>Biomarkers of oxidative damage</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-bt6jm"><span><span>Mitochondria are particularly vulnerable to damage because their DNA lacks two self-protection mechanisms employed by nuclear DNA: histone proteins and efficient DNA repair mechanisms. Mitochondria become a target of their own production. ROS can cause nicks, breaks and mutations in mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA). A region of mtDNA, the Displacement or D-loop, is a hotspot for DNA damage. Several mutations occurring here are associated with cancers. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-93eg6"><span><span>Damaged DNA is poorly amplified by PCR, so the extent of damage can be measured by degree of amplification.<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup> Other markers of oxidative damage include </span><strong><span>8-OH-dG </span></strong><span>(modified nucleotides), </span><strong><span><span>γ</span></span></strong><strong><span>H2AX </span></strong><span>(forms on histones when double-strand breaks occur), </span><strong><span>MDA</span></strong><span> (malondialdehyde), and </span><strong><span>HNE</span></strong><span> (one of the most common aldehyde biomarkers of lipid peroxidation).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-flli9"><span id="antioxidants-as-disease-therapeutics-flli9"></span><span><span>Antioxidants as disease therapeutics</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-c09el"><span><span>Cell damage caused by free radicals underlies aging, cancer, Alzheimer's, coronary heart (CV) disease, cataracts, immune system decline, liver diseases, diabetes mellitus, inflammation, renal failure, brain dysfunction, and stress.<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-6tipv"><span><span>Replenishing antioxidants can help protect against oxidative stress while promoting the health of metabolically-demanding tissues, such as the brain, joints, eyes, and skin. This is because antioxidants are typically stable resonance structures with delocalized electrons that can afford to provide an electron and become positively charged.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-243vu"><span id="what-are-the-key-nutrients-found-in-mushrooms-243vu"></span><span><span>What are the key nutrients found in mushrooms?</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-cgr9b"><span><span>Mushrooms are an excellent source of many vitamins, minerals, and other nutrients. id="fa29ad_e8f0e4a735a942969a9c564cecc53764~mv2.jpg"><img alt="brown button baby bella mushrooms" loading="lazy" src="/images/ergothioneine/10_fa29ad-e8f0e4a735a942969a9c564cecc53764-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div><div id="viewer-c95ff-caption"><div><figcaption><span>White button, brown button (baby bella), cremini, and portobella mushrooms are all the same species (Agaricus bisporus) at different stages of ripening. White buttons are the youngest.</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-dacn"><span><span>Plants with the highest levels of ergothioneine include tempeh, asparagus, and garlic. Trace amounts can be found in basil, tofu, cumin, pepper, red and black beans, Japanese seaweed, kiwi, various nuts (pistachios, almonds), oats, pomegranate, passion fruit, onions, and durian. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-e7f8f"><span><span>But even so, most of these plants contain between 1-5 mg/kg of EGT. By comparison, </span><em><span>Boletus edulis</span></em><span> (porcini mushroom) contains 1,812.4 mg/kg dry weight of ergothioneine. Shiitake mushrooms contain over 10 times more ergothioneine than garlic.<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-9ldtm"><span id="mechanism-of-action-of-egt-9ldtm"></span><span><span>Mechanism of Action of EGT</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-9c97l"><span><span>How does ergothioneine work? Several research studies have demonstrated EGT's potential mechanisms of action:</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-3bu44"><span id="protects-dna-and-proteins-3bu44"></span><span><span>Protects DNA and proteins</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-boomp"><span><span>EGT scavenges and quenches free radicals, dose-dependently prevents DNA breaks and mutations, and prevents oxidation and carbonylation of proteins.<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-8vu7h"><span id="cytokine-inhibitor-8vu7h"></span><span><span>Cytokine inhibitor</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-83dp3"><span><span>EGT was shown to inhibits pro-inflammatory cytokines like IL-6, IL-8 and TNF-α in alveolar macrophages and epithelial cell cultures.<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a>,<a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-6qfou"><span id="apoptosis-inhibitor-6qfou"></span><span><span>Apoptosis inhibitor</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-aiklu"><span><span>EGT is cytoprotective. It inhibits apoptosis and promotes growth by activating p38 MAPK and JNK signaling pathways.<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-d22dc"><span id="glutathione-upregulator-d22dc"></span><span><span>Glutathione upregulator</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-cd2do"><span><span>EGT can induce synthesis of glutathione by induction of the Nrf2/ARE-mediated signaling pathway.<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-81u7f"><span id="metal-chelator-81u7f"></span><span><span>Metal chelator</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-5k9a7"><span><span>EGT chelates metal ions, sequestering them from generating ROS. EGT form complexes with copper (Cu2+), iron (Fe2+), zinc (Zn2+), cobalt (Co2+), mercury (Hg2+), cadmium (Cd2+), and nickel (Ni2+), the most stable being the copper complex. This metal chelation activity protects against DNA and oxidative damage. EGT was shown to protect sperm motility from the harmful effects of copper.<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-4rshb"><span><span>In fact, whereas other low molecular weight thiols in aerobic organisms are susceptible to metal-catalyzed autooxidation, the sulfur atom of EGT coordinates metal ions, thereby forming a non-redox active complex composed of two molecules of ergothioneine and one metal ion.<sup><a href="#ref-15">15</a></sup></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-6rb99"><span id="therapeutic-effects-of-egt-6rb99"></span><span><span>Therapeutic Effects of EGT</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-beha9"><span><span>Ergothioneine has demonstrated therapeutic potential for gut dysbiosis, skin aging, ischemia-reperfusion injuries, Parkinson's disease, and Alzheimer's disease. Based on cell culture and animal model research, it has been suggested to be therapeutic in other conditions, but clinical trials are needed to directly establish its safety and efficacy.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-bogea"><span id="cataracts-bogea"></span><span><span>Cataracts</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-omg1"><span><span>Oxidative damage can occur in the eyes because of their high exposure to oxygen; this process underlies eye disease. Substantial EGT is found in the eyes and is decreased in the lens and corneas of patients with cataracts.<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup> EGT could help counteract eye aging.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-aevqb"><span id="promotes-heart-health-aevqb"></span><span><span>Promotes heart health</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-6bh4i"><span><span>EGT is found in high amounts in the blood and travels readily to the heart, where it neutralizes ROS and chelates metals. It may be therapeutic in myocardial ischemia-reperfusion injuries.<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-7ilds"><span id="kidney-disease-7ilds"></span><span><span>Kidney disease</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-flj4j"><span><span>EGT is lowered in the kidneys of patients with chronic kidney disease. Knocking out OCTN1 in mice increased severity of renal fibrosis, manifested as oxidative damage.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-ekll2"><span id="diabetes-ekll2"></span><span><span>Diabetes</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7ufr7"><span><span>Diabetes is linked to oxidative and glycative stress. EGT may help counteract the pro-oxidant effect of hyperglycemia. Maitake mushrooms have been shown to be beneficial for patients with diabetes, as they improve glucose tolerance.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-fecfv"><div><figure><div><div id="fecfv"><wow-image id="fa29ad_5b676e491d744e58b1b6a6c83e921480~mv2.jpg"><img alt="Maitake mushrooms" loading="lazy" src="/images/ergothioneine/04_fa29ad-5b676e491d744e58b1b6a6c83e921480-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div><div id="viewer-fecfv-caption"><div><figcaption><span>Roasted maitake mushrooms</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-1gv9b"><span id="anti-aging-for-skin-1gv9b"></span><span><span>Anti-aging for skin</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7cka7"><span><span>EGT directly absorbs UV radiation and dose-dependently prevents DNA breaks and mutations.<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup> EGT decreased caspase-9 (apoptotic signal) activity in keratinocytes exposed to UV radiation.<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup></span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-237dp"><span id="neuroprotective-237dp"></span><span><span>Neuroprotective</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-dajb6"><span><span>Blood EGT levels significantly decline after age 60. Subjects with mild cognitive impairment, as well as patients with Parkinson’s, had significantly lower plasma EGT levels compared with age-matched subjects.<sup><a href="#ref-10">10</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-6rvk4"><span><span>Multiple studies support EGT’s neuroprotective activity in Alzheimer's disease.<sup><a href="#ref-11">11</a></sup> EGT significantly prevented Aβ accumulation in the hippocampus and brain lipid peroxidation in mice. EGT also restored cognitive function and inhibited cisplatin-induced lipid peroxidation.<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup> Yang et. al showed EGT can protect against Aβ-induced loss of memory and learning abilities in mice.<sup><a href="#ref-12">12</a></sup> EGT was also neuroprotective against NMDA excitotoxicity in retinal rat models.<sup><a href="#ref-13">13</a></sup> Further studies are needed to establish whether EGT has a therapeutic effect on Parkinson's and Alzheimer's disease in humans.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-8fnij"><span id="may-play-a-role-in-gut-health-8fnij"></span><span><span>May play a role in gut health</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-3l14o"><span><span>Ergothioneine levels are significantly lower in dysbiosis mice. On the other hand, elevated EGT concentrations have been observed in injured tissues, particularly liver, heart, joint, and intestinal injury.<sup><a href="#ref-14">14</a></sup> This suggests EGT could dose-dependently promote gut homeostasis and suppress inflammation. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-2rtv2"><span id="safety-toxicity-and-potential-contraindications-2rtv2"></span><span><span>Safety, toxicity, and potential contraindications</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-9tkel"><span><span>Ergothioneine has European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) approval in the EU and is generally recognized as safe (GRAS) by the FDA as a supplement.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5gb7q"><span><span>Certain fungi and microorganisms make use of EGT to protect themselves from attack by host defenses (ROS/RNS). It's important to realize that diseased cells (i.e. cancer cells or abnormal immune cells) may also use EGT to promote their survival. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-ae4gg"><span><span>Research has suggested EGT is contraindicated for patients with cancer, Crohn’s disease, and rheumatoid arthritis.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-dpl5b"><span id="cancer-dpl5b"></span><span><span>Cancer</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-5uqg6"><span><span>Significantly increased OCTN1 mRNA was found in thyroid, liver, and esophageal cancers. It's possible cancer cells accumulate ergothioneine to fight off/protect against the oxidative stress generated by chemotherapy. Possible therapeutic targets for cancer include depleting EGT and/or blocking the transporter.<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup> </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-84b03"><span><span>This doesn't rule out ergothioneine's potential to prevent cancer. Research is contradictory, as many mushrooms are well-recognized for their ability to fight cancer. Overall, further research is necessary to establish its activity and role in cancer.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-e0fni"><span id="crohns-disease-and-rheumatoid-arthritis-e0fni"></span><span><span>Crohn's disease and rheumatoid arthritis</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7rhe7"><span><span>Crohn's disease and rheumatoid arthritis are two inflammatory conditions that both demonstrate alterations in the OCTN1 transporter. EGT is suggested to promote survival of inflamed tissues, thereby indirectly contributing to inflammation.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-6iegs"><span id="compromised-immunity-6iegs"></span><span><span>Compromised immunity</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-c55nv"><span><span>Individuals with compromised immune systems have been advised to avoid EGT.<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup> Certain anti-inflammatory medications taken for autoimmune diseases can also compromise immunity.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-fe194"><span id="conclusion-fe194"></span><span><span>Conclusion</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-edj9p"><span><span>Many gaps in the research remain when it comes to ergothioneine efficacy. EGT appears to be a safe, diet-derived antioxidant with promising therapeutic potential, but double-blind, placebo-controlled studies are yet to be conducted.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-7qe1e"><span><span>EGT concentrates in areas of oxidative stress. Its many intriguing properties, such as its presence in infants, absorption of UV radiation, and.metal chelating abilities point to its value in preventive medicine.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-1vrf0"><span><span>Health status should be considered before introducing a new supplement into the diet. It's important to consult your doctor before incorporating an ergothioneine supplement. A nutritionist can also provide more personalized advice. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-bkbps"><span><span>Overall, mushrooms offer a variety of antioxidants which confer many impressive health benefits.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-14mmd"><span id="further-reading-14mmd"></span><span><span>Further Reading</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-bhb0d"><span><span>Whitmore, Clayton A et al. “Longitudinal Consumption of Ergothioneine Reduces Oxidative Stress and Amyloid Plaques and Restores Glucose Metabolism in the 5XFAD Mouse Model of Alzheimer's Disease.” </span><em><span>Pharmaceuticals (Basel, Switzerland)</span></em><span> vol. 15,6 742. 13 Jun. </span><strong><span>2022</span></strong><span>, doi:10.3390/ph15060742</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-ckhp8"><span><span>Wijesinghe, Printha et al. “Ergothioneine, a dietary antioxidant improves amyloid beta clearance in the neuroretina of a mouse model of Alzheimer's disease.” </span><em><span>Frontiers in neuroscience</span></em><span> vol. 17 1107436. 14 Mar. </span><strong><span>2023</span></strong><span>, doi:10.3389/fnins.2023.1107436</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><details class="references"><summary><span class="ref-caret" aria-hidden="true"></span><h2 id="viewer-b6cod"><span id="references-b6cod"></span><span><span>References</span></span></h2></summary><div type="heading"></div><div><ol><li id="ref-1"><p id="viewer-foesr"><span><span>Paul, B.D. and S.H.
