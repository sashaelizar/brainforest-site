id="viewer-daa4d"><span id="1-food-allergies-and-intolerances-daa4d"></span><span><span>1. Food allergies and intolerances</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><div id="viewer-71s61"><blockquote><span><div id="viewer-ksvd84904490"><span><span>"One man's food is another man's poison."</span></span></div></span></blockquote></div></div><div type="blockquote"></div><div><p id="viewer-66l7g"><span><span>It's estimated that up to 20% of the world's population may have a food intolerance.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-9eitg"><span><span>A diet that one person swears by may sicken another and nearly land them in the hospital.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5sq2a"><span><span>What one person deems a superfood can give another person skin breakouts.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-of2p"><span><span>You can develop allergies over your life, such that something that never used to bother you now gives you noticeable symptoms.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-ki2m"><span><span>Keeping a food log can help you keep track of how your body responds to certain foods.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-bs67d"><span id="2-plant-toxins-bs67d"></span><span><span>2. Plant toxins</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-f7q40"><span><span>An estimated 96% of Americans are omnivores. Recent archaeological evidence shows that earlier in human evolution, meat consumption, specifically in the form of fat-rich </span><a href="https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2019/04/a-marrow-advantage/586444/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>bone marrow</span></u></span></a><span><u><span>,</span></u></span><span> catalyzed the cortical expansion.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-amval"><span><span>Further, plants evolved mechanisms to defend themselves from being eaten, which can be toxic to humans. This may explain why many people experience worsening health such as brain fog, bloating, and decreased muscle mass on a raw vegan diet. There are oxalates, lectins, FODMAPs, antinutrients like tannins and phytic acid that decrease absorption of minerals, and much more.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-ckl1s"><div><figure><a href="https://gundrymd.com/reduce-lectins-diet/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><div><div id="ckl1s"><wow-image id="fa29ad_06ea343f22b745abb56fb91f2c86c6f4~mv2.jpg"><img alt="Lectins affect the gut" loading="lazy" src="/images/there-is-no-one-diet/01_fa29ad-06ea343f22b745abb56fb91f2c86c6f4-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></a><div id="viewer-ckl1s-caption"><div><figcaption><span>Source: Gundry MD</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-83ltn"><span><a href="https://www.upmc.com/-/media/upmc/patients-visitors/education/unique-pdfs/low-oxalate-diet.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><strong><span><u><span>Oxalate</span></u></span></strong></a><strong><span>-rich</span></strong><span> foods like spinach, almonds, and chocolate can stress out the kidneys.</span><strong><span> Lectins</span></strong><span> found in tomatoes, eggplant, and peppers can cause leaky gut in those predisposed. </span><strong><span>FODMAPs,</span></strong><span> which stands for fermentable oligo-, di-, mono-saccharides and poly-ols, are short-chain carbohydrates that are poorly absorbed in the small intestine. Fiber does not help with bloating and is not recommended for IBS or SIBO.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-5l3nj"><span><span>Certain people may be sensitive to particular plant toxins and have to avoid certain fruits, vegetables, grains, legumes, nuts, or seeds accordingly.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-7kpe6"><span id="3-ancestry-and-genetics-7kpe6"></span><span><span>3. Ancestry and genetics</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-3b884"><span><span>Geography, climate, and available food sources shape dietary choices, which affects protein expression profiles. For example, certain groups can digest milk better than others due to lactase persistence.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-ce403"><div><figure><div><div id="ce403"><wow-image id="fa29ad_0a7f427093894ceba5351c81ac5bb7af~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/there-is-no-one-diet/02_fa29ad-0a7f427093894ceba5351c81ac5bb7af-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-99ovp"><span><span>If someone has a family history of diabetes and kidney issues, they may restrict intake of grains and sugars, while someone with a family history of heart disease might be more cautious about eating meat.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-catdj"><span id="4-different-effects-imply-different-requirements-catdj"></span><span><span>4. Different effects imply different requirements</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-div2k"><span><span>Some people get brain fog from eating certain kinds of meat, like turkey, and others get brain fog from completely cutting out meat, due to vitamin B12 deficiency or lack of some other nutritional requirement.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-c0gm9"><span id="5-performance-dependent-c0gm9"></span><span><span>5. Performance-dependent</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-fjuuf"><span><span>Elite athletes training for a marathon or extreme endurance may eat mostly protein and simple sugars (e.g. chicken, pasta, banana) and stay away from fatty foods and fiber (e.g. fish, beans, broccoli). This is because fats and fiber break down more slowly, and more blood will go to the gut, decreasing muscle performance. Carbs are also a quick source of energy. A high-carb diet may work for a triathlete but may be potentially disastrous for a type 2 diabetic patient.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-42sbe"><span id="6-metabolic-rate-affects-appetite-42sbe"></span><span><span>6. Metabolic rate affects appetite</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7rrv3"><span><span>The food you eat and when you eat it affects your metabolism and subsequently your hunger.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-8hlqf"><span><span>To speed up your metabolism, move around, be active, eat protein, do cardio, lift weights, and get a good night's sleep. Caloric restriction, sedentary lifestyle, and delaying breakfast generally slow down metabolism.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-fmmda"><span><span>Sugars and high-glycemic foods spike blood glucose faster, so they may not hold you over as long.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-2g15a"><span id="7-gut-microbes-affect-our-food-behaviors-preferences-aversions-and-cravings-2g15a"></span><span><span>7. Gut microbes affect our food behaviors, preferences, aversions, and cravings</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-2ttnl"><span><span>Diet impacts which </span><a href="https://www.infiniteemergence.com/post/ecosystem-gut-microbiota" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>gut microbiota</span></u></span></a><span> thrive in the gut, and in turn, gut microbes influence sensations like satiety, nausea, and conditioned food aversion, creating a feedback loop of dietary preferences. Around 90% of the body's serotonin and </span><a href="https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fimmu.2018.02398/full" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>50% of the body's dopamine</span></u></span></a><span> is produced in the gut, emphasizing the role of gut microbes in food-related mood and reward pathways.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-9erl5"><div><figure><div><div id="9erl5"><wow-image id="fa29ad_fb5c2929ca7d4b2db78f8be26287e97b~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/there-is-no-one-diet/03_fa29ad-fb5c2929ca7d4b2db78f8be26287e97b-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><p id="viewer-5e1lj"><span><span>Since gut microbes modulate the </span><strong><span>vagus nerve,</span></strong><span> part of the autonomic nervous system, a lot of our response to food is not conscious. A person who once enjoyed the taste of greasy food could later find the smell of fried fast food nauseating because of the gut's memory of that food wrecking their digestion, or simply because their gut microbial profile has changed. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-1qe71"><span><span>Intuitive eating goes beyond the taste buds' addiction to sugar, salt, and fat, and it considers how we feel minutes to hours to days after we eat.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-8d670"><span id="8-sustainability-of-restrictive-vs-diversified-diets-8d670"></span><span><span>8. Sustainability of restrictive vs. diversified diets</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-81a90"><span><span>Many people grow bored of eating the same thing every day, week, or month. This makes it harder to draw conclusions from diet data. Omnivores become semi-vegetarians, vegans become pescatarians, people cut out gluten or dairy for health reasons. Seeking out the nutrients our bodies need from diverse sources helps keep dieting enjoyable rather than restrictive.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-6avi"><span id="9-chronic-and-infectious-diseases-modulate-gut-blood-brain-integrity-6avi"></span><span><span>9. Chronic and infectious diseases modulate gut-blood-brain integrity</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-19vqf"><span><span>Certain disease statuses can alter the microbial composition and create a need for specific dietary interventions tailored for each case.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-2gc0"><span><span>For instance, most people are recommended fiber-rich diets for the short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) metabolites and their effects on immunity and brain health. However, fiber may upset digestion and exacerbate inflammation in some people, including those with IBD and Crohn's. It's important to note that fiber isn't causing the disease. IBD patients may see improvement of symptoms on a low fiber diet due to a different gut microbial profile, and they should read about the </span><a href="https://chriskresser.com/fodmaps-could-common-foods-be-harming-your-digestive-health/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>low FODMAP diet</span></u></span></a><span>, specific carbohydrate diet (</span><a href="https://healthygut.com/about-the-scd-diet/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>SCD</span></u></span></a><span>), and the </span><a href="http://www.gapsdiet.com/gaps-full-diet.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>GAPS diet</span></u></span></a><span>.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-7v698"><span><span>Patients with a compromised blood-brain barrier due to leaky gut, autoimmunity, or active viruses should be take caution when consuming metal chelators such as chlorella, spirulina, or cilantro, because it can redistribute heavy metals like mercury to the brain. People without these conditions don't need to be as concerned with avoiding these particular foods. </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-cmd7"><span><span>I recommend taking a gut micribiome test, such as </span><a href="http://viome.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Viome</span></u></span></a><span>, which reveals patterns in biochemical pathways and can tell you if your gut lining is compromised and which viruses may exist in your gut.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-9hqcb"><span id="10-lifelong-variations-9hqcb"></span><span><span>10. Lifelong variations</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-70shh"><span><span>Our diet varies continuously throughout our lives and even on a daily basis. What we want and need can change over time. We seek out different foods at age 10, 20, 40, and 60. Kids have more sensitive taste buds and high energy, often leading them to prefer bland and carbohydrate-rich foods like mac 'n' cheese. What a 20-year-old finds salty, a 50-year-old may find perfectly seasoned.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-2em2p"><span id="11-the-nutritional-entourage-effect-2em2p"></span><span><span>11. The nutritional entourage effect </span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-b9tdl"><span><span>Many studies comparing diets contradict each other. They don't take into account the complex interplay between activity levels, food type, caloric intake, timing of eating, genetics and gene expression (intestinal transporters, immune biomarkers), metabolic rate, nutritional status, and gut microbial composition.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-8femq"><span><span>Several studies show that different low-fat and low-carb diets </span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28214525" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>don't show a significant difference</span></u></span></a><span> in weight loss. More people drop off low-carb diets like keto, likely due to our body's preference to use glucose as fuel—the restriction can even cause weight to rebound and overshoot. It seems to be more important </span><em><span>where</span></em><span> we are sourcing these nutrients from. Specific foods seem to matter because of synergistic interactions between their constituent molecules, known as the entourage effect. Supplements may not work for some people because they lack nutritional diversity, enter in the wrong part of the pathway (e.g. too far downstream and not at the root cause), lack bioactivity, have unwanted side effects, are not compatible with host biochemistry, are too high of a dose, or contain toxic contaminants.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-2q93u"><span id="key-takeaway-2q93u"></span><span><span>Key takeaway</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-52ucg"><span><span>Factors that contribute to the optimal diet at a given time include: </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><ul><li><p id="viewer-3o5lh"><span><span>individual genetics</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-8qsbt"><span><span>gene expression, e.g. intestinal transporters and receptors</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-anifu"><span><span>food behaviors: food preferences, timing of eating, caloric intake</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-3q0d"><span><span>nutritional quality and food purity, including soil quality where food is grown</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-990b1"><span><span>endocrine factors: gut microbial composition, hormones</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-6ghu1"><span><span>immune biomarkers: inflammation, infections, autoimmunity</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-f68kq"><span><span>metabolic rate</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-gl8h"><span><span>activity and energy levels</span></span></p></li><li><p id="viewer-1s08g"><span><span>body composition</span></span></p></li></ul></div><div type="bulleted_list"></div><div></div></div><div type="last"></div></div></div></div></div></div></section>
