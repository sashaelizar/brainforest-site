Besides its use as a spice, food preservative, and dye, turmeric has been traditionally used in Ayurvedic medicine in India, China, and Southeast Asia for nearly 4,000 years in the treatment of various ailments such as arthritis, ulcers, jaundice, wounds, fever, trauma, and psoriasis (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3025067/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Zhou, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-c8i3b"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><p id="viewer-24b9b"><span><span>Curcumin, a major component of turmeric, is a potent anti-inflammatory antioxidant. The targets of curcumin include transcription factors like Wnt and Notch (which promote neuronal development), growth factors, protein kinases, inflammatory cytokines, enzymes, adhesion molecules, and apoptotic proteins </span><span><u><span>(</span></u></span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3025067/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Zhou, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-ebp50"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><p id="viewer-4p624"><span><span>Curcumin is highly safe and efficacious. It exhibits anti-inflammatory effects, antioxidant activity, promotes mitochondrial function, accelerates wound healing, protects the liver, gut, brain, and heart, and possesses anti-cancer properties. Despite its safety, several studies note that beneficial effects plateau beyond a dose of 8 grams per day.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-6vodi"><span id="benefits-of-curcumin-6vodi"></span><span><span>Benefits of Curcumin</span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-d1g25"><span><span>Curcumin has demonstrated striking clinical benefits for Alzheimer's disease, anxiety, brain tumors, obesity, gingivitis, and rheumatoid arthritis. Curcumin is also anti-inflammatory in microglia and astrocytes and improves cardiovascular function.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-67nlj"><span id="section-67nlj"></span><span><br/></span></h3></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-f31t8"><span id="1-anti-inflammatory-f31t8"></span><span><span>1. Anti-inflammatory</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-d3vf4"><span><span>Curcumin inhibits the NF‐κB transcription factor, COX‐2 enzyme, and reactive oxygen species (ROS) production. Curcumin downregulates various pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as TNF‐α, IFN-gamma, IL‐1, IL‐6, IL‐8, IL‐12, CCL2, and IL‐1β (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5429333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kunnumakkara, 2017</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin can also inhibit activated microglia and astrocytes (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6032333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Maiti, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). It also accelerates the wound healing process by inducing apoptosis of inflammatory cells (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6429075/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Barchitta, 2019</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-e0asn"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-3nd5m"><span id="2-hepatoprotective-3nd5m"></span><span><span>2. Hepatoprotective</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-3hh1k"><span><span>Alcohol, drugs, viral infections, environmental pollutants, and dietary components can cause liver damage. Curcumin inhibits oxidative stress, free radical formation, and DNA damage in the liver. Treatment of 100 mg/kg of curcumin intraperitoneally in mice downregulated NOS-2, decreasing nitric oxide (NO) and lipid peroxidation products in the liver. (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6073929/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Farzaei, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-3oe7b"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><div id="viewer-7uump"><div><figure><div><div id="7uump"><wow-image id="fa29ad_cdff53bee4ae47718b352a62212448e9~mv2.jpg"><img alt="turmeric plant" loading="lazy" src="/images/9-curcumin-benefits/06_fa29ad-cdff53bee4ae47718b352a62212448e9-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div><div id="viewer-7uump-caption"><div><figcaption><span>Curcuma longa plant</span></figcaption></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-3eda3"><span id="3-neuroprotective-3eda3"></span><span><span>3. Neuroprotective</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-7jp40"><span><span>Curcumin can cross and stabilize the blood-brain barrier. It is one of the strongest inhibitors of amyloid-beta aggregation compared to numerous antioxidants, but it is not widely used in Western medicine in part due to doubts about its profitability (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/26035622/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Hu, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). South Asian countries where curcumin is used daily tend to have lower rates of Alzheimer's disease than Western countries, where curcumin consumption is lower on average (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6032333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Maiti, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-bgtnk"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h4 id="viewer-89eq5"><span id="alzheimers-disease-89eq5"></span><span><strong><em><span>Alzheimer's disease </span></em></strong></span></h4></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-3ckdv"><span><span>Curcumin is a BACE inhibitor of the amyloid precursor protein (APP) pathway. In vitro, curcumin attenuates hyperphosphorylation of tau (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24188406/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Huang, 2014</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin also inhibits Aβ and tau accumulation and enhances mitochondria and synaptic function in animal models (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6210685/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kim, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). An 18-month, randomized clinical trial involving 40 patients indicated that curcumin could reduce amyloid (Aβ) and tau accumulation, leading to improved memory and attention in adults without dementia compared to controls (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/29246725/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Small, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). Overall, curcumin is effective and delays disease progression in elderly individuals, but it is less effective in patients with severe Alzheimer's (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5796761/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Reddy, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). Strikingly, curcumin has been shown to stimulate neuronal stem cell proliferation, neuronal differentiation, and hippocampal neurogenesis by activating the Wnt/β-catenin pathway in AD models (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/24467380/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Tiwari, 2014</span></u></span></a><span>). These data suggest curcumin can prevent or delay disease progression, but may not reverse late-stage AD.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-3919j"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h4 id="viewer-3ono0"><span id="parkinsons-disease-3ono0"></span><span><strong><em><span>Parkinson's disease </span></em></strong></span></h4></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-4kcae"><span><span>Curcumin chelates iron, copper, and other metals, thus preventing α-synuclein or Lewy body aggregation. It promotes macroautophagy, reducing cell death and neurotoxicity. It acts like an MAOI, restoring dopamine levels and reducing depression. It can also protect dopaminergic neurons by reducing ROS levels, maintaining mitochondrial functions, attenuating neuroinflammation, and preventing apoptosis (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6032333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Maiti, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-678lj"></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><h4 id="viewer-dackv"><span id="huntingtons-disease-dackv"></span><span><strong><em><span>Huntington's disease </span></em></strong></span></h4></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-2npo3"><span><span>In Huntington's disease models, curcumin restored glutathione levels and stimulated superoxide dismutase, an antioxidant enzyme (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6032333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Maiti, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-3v7vs"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h4 id="viewer-444d7"><span id="depression-and-anxiety-444d7"></span><span><strong><em><span>Depression and anxiety </span></em></strong></span></h4></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-8ctm3"><span><span>Curcumin was shown to reduce anxiety in obese patients after 30 days, but it had no effect on depression (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25776839" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Esmaily, 2015</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin also improves survival of neurons and promotes neurogenesis by increasing Notch signaling (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/27320251/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Liu, 2016</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-8ckj3"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><div id="viewer-t7tp"><div><figure><div><div id="t7tp"><wow-image id="fa29ad_03394d7c07334e8ea8aae6826bae6970~mv2.jpg"><img alt="" loading="lazy" src="/images/9-curcumin-benefits/01_fa29ad-03394d7c07334e8ea8aae6826bae6970-.jpg"/></wow-image></div></div></figure></div></div></div><div type="image"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-e6l94"><span id="4-anti-cancer-e6l94"></span><span><span>4. Anti-Cancer</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-2uvie"><span><span>Curcumin targets pathways associated with tumorigenesis, cell proliferation, angiogenesis, metastasis, apoptosis, and cell cycle regulation (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4334672/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Waghela, 2015</span></u></span></a><span> &amp; </span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3533886/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Hasima, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>). It inhibits expression of Bcl2, a pro-survival protein that prevents </span><em><span>cytochrome c</span></em><span> release in cancer cells, allowing them to be killed more easily (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1891180/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Sagar, 2006</span></u></span></a><span>). It has also been shown to inhibit the mTOR pathway in several cancers. Curcumin also targeted integrins, a type of cell adhesion molecule, which helped prevent metastasis in </span><strong><span>melanoma</span></strong><span> cells (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12678405/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Ray, 2003</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-1fvis"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><p id="viewer-8kan0"><span><span>Curcumin was found to inhibit proliferation and induce apoptosis in certain </span><strong><span>glioblastoma</span></strong><span> tumors (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4952019/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Rodriguez, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). It has also enhanced p53 and caspase signaling and promoted apoptosis in </span><strong><span>colon cancer</span></strong><span> cells (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4334672/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Waghela, 2015</span></u></span></a><span>). Several studies saw decreased proliferation of </span><strong><span>breast cancer</span></strong><span> cells when treated with curcumin (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3533886/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Hasima, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin's downregulation of NF-KB, a transcription factor that is constitutively expressed in numerous cancer cell lines and tumors, is suspected to be a major reason for its efficacy against </span><strong><span>pancreatic cancer</span></strong><span> (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4110570/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kanai, 2014</span></u></span></a><span> &amp; </span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3025067/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Zhou, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>). However, these were in vitro studies, and clinical trials are needed.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-9on6k"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><p id="viewer-4ar7j"><span><span>It has been suggested that curcumin could reduce the need for chemotherapeutic drugs. Pretreatment of HeLa cells with 5 μM curcumin lowered the amount of paclitaxel needed to kill </span><strong><span>cervical cancer</span></strong><span> cells. Treatment with curcumin increased vincristine's cytotoxicity from less than 10% to over 70% in a </span><strong><span>multiple myeloma</span></strong><span> cell line. It did this by increasing caspases, inhibiting p65 nuclear localization, and downregulating NF-KB (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/12393461/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Bharti, 2003</span></u></span></a><span>). Finally, pediatric patients with relapsed </span><strong><span>brain tumors</span></strong><span> undergoing chemotherapy who were treated with curcumin had increased response compared to controls (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5429333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kunnumakkara, 2017</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-dk79e"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-7n37g"><span id="5-improves-gut-health-7n37g"></span><span><span>5. Improves gut health</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-dvj8d"><span><span>Rats on a high-fat diet administrated curcumin showed enhanced diversity of gut bacteria (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6210685/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kim, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). In a double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot study of 14 patients, curcumin was found to increase biodiversity of gut microbiota (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6083746/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Peterson, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin has been shown to increase </span><em><span>Lactobacillus </span></em><span>and </span><em><span>Bifidobacterium </span></em><span>families and increase the abundance of butyrate-producing bacteria (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6311836/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Zam, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) like butyrate are highly effective at reducing inflammation.</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-479m3"><span><span>Curcumin was shown to reduce gut permeability by activating MPK-1, a protein that inactivates MAPK, p38, and NF-KB (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6210685/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kim, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). Curcumin reduced ulcerative colitis relapses (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/17101300" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Hanai, 2016</span></u></span></a><span>). Another pilot study found that 4 out of 5 Crohn's disease patients saw an improvement of scores (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5429333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kunnumakkara, 2017</span></u></span></a><span>). </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-6b5f7"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-3voco"><span id="6-helps-with-obesity-3voco"></span><span><span>6. Helps with obesity</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-5ksgk"><span><span>Curcumin improves glucose and lipid metabolism and facilitates insulin signaling. Curcumin mitigates inflammatory effects of obesity on the gut (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6210685/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kim, 2018</span></u></span></a><span>). It does this by decreasing macrophage infiltration into adipocytes, endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress, and SIRT1 expression in adipocytes.  In a 12-week, double-blind, randomized control study, a group of 118 obese patients were assigned to 1 gram of curcumin plus 10 mg of piperine (a bioavailability-enhancing compound) a day. Obese patients showed an increase in 'good' HDL cholesterol and a decrease in BMI and body weight. No change was observed in LDL cholesterol. (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/28735818/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Panahi, 2017</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-d7l8o"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-6oo3e"><span id="7-improves-cardiovascular-health-6oo3e"></span><span><span>7. Improves cardiovascular health</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-6lvvq"><span><span>Consumption of curry spices rich in antioxidants, like curcumin and eugenol, improves post-meal endothelial function in healthy male subjects, which is beneficial for cardiovascular health. The participants who ate curry had an increased flow‐mediated vasodilatation response (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5429333/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Kunnumakkara, 2017</span></u></span></a><span>). </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-2n55l"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h3 id="viewer-1gfgs"><span id="8-fights-off-gingivitis-1gfgs"></span><span><span>8. Fights off gingivitis</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-34alk"><span><span>Curcumin mouthwash was found to be almost as good as prescription chlorohexidine (CHX) mouthwash. Ameliorates arthritis and joint inflammation</span></span></h3></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-84agc"><span><span>A pilot clinical study investigating the safety and efficacy of curcumin in active rheumatoid arthritis patients showed an improvement in disease scores. Curcumin treatment was also found to reduce the stiffness and swelling in the joints of patients with rheumatoid arthritis (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22407780" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Chandran, 2012</span></u></span></a><span>).</span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><div id="viewer-63aoo"><span><br/></span></div></div><div type="empty-line"></div><div><h2 id="viewer-1otcc"><span id="promoting-curcumin-absorption-and-bioavailability-1otcc"></span><span><span>Promoting curcumin absorption and bioavailability </span></span></h2></div><div type="heading"></div><div><p id="viewer-1cc83"><span><span>The safety and efficacy of curcumin is well-established, but pharmacokinetic studies reveal its bioavailability as a barrier. This is due to curcumin's instability, poor solubility and absorption, and rapid metabolism and excretion (</span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6073929/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Farzaei, 2018</span></u></span></a><span> &amp; </span><a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3788358/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span><u><span>Vyas, 2013</span></u></span></a><span>). </span></span></p></div><div type="paragraph"></div><div><p id="viewer-6e8k0"><span><span>Efforts to improve the bioavailability of curcumin have met success.
